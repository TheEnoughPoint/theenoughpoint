Singapore savers have more places to park spare cash than at almost any point in the last decade — T-bill auctions, Singapore Savings Bonds, and a growing set of cash management platforms all compete for money that used to sit in a savings account earning close to nothing. More choice is generally good, but it also means the "best" option depends heavily on how you weigh a few specific trade-offs.

## The Trade-offs That Actually Matter

**Access speed.** T-bills and SSBs settle on a schedule tied to auction and redemption dates — your money isn't available on demand. Cash management platforms are typically built for same- or next-business-day access, at the cost of a small chance of NAV fluctuation.

**Yield certainty.** A T-bill's yield is fixed at the point of allotment for its full tenor. Cash management portfolio yields move with the underlying money market rates and are not fixed in advance — the figure you see today is a recent yield, not a locked-in rate.

**Minimums and friction.** T-bill applications go through a bank or brokerage and involve a bidding process; cash management accounts are generally simpler to open and top up, which matters more than it sounds like for money you want to move often.

## How to Think About the Choice

None of this is a suggestion that one platform — or T-bills, or SSBs — is the "right" answer. A reasonable, hypothetical approach some households use is to ladder: a portion in T-bills or SSBs for money with a known timeline, and a portion in a cash management platform for money that needs to stay flexible. The split is a personal judgment call based on how predictable your near-term cash needs actually are, not a formula to copy directly.
